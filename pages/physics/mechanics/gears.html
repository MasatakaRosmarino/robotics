gears and gear trains

Principle

Gears follow the same principle that class 1 levers follow, they rotate around an axle (the fulchrum), and one side exerts a downwards or upwards force, while the opposite side exerts the same force in the opposite direction.

They can be distinguished by their radius or number of teeth.

Simple gears

Simple gears are defined simply by their number of teeth and radius

Compound gears

Compound gears are defined by their number of inner and outer teeth and radii

gear trains

Gears can be coupled together to form gear trains; this arrangement enables to adjust the torque and rotation speed to create mechanical advantage.

there is always a drive gear that gives the input force, for example the gear attached to a DC motor axle, and a driven gear, that exerts the output force.

Smaller gears are normally called pinions, whereas larger gears are called gears.

The key factor is the gear ratio, which is calculated with the formula: driven gear : drive gear.

For example, if the drive gear has 10 teeth, and the driven gear has 20 teeth, the gear ratio is:
20 : 10 = 2
.

This means that:

  • for every turn of the driven gear, the drive gear turns 2 times as much.
  • The mechanical advantage is 2 times the input force from the drive gear.
